The article argues that organizations are making the same mistake early factory owners made with electricity: bolting new technology onto systems designed for humans rather than redesigning around it. Drawing parallels to how factories had to be physically redesigned to capitalize on electric power, the author contends that AI agents require similar structural changes. Organizations must convert knowledge into machine-readable formats (plain-text markdown instead of PDFs), expose system capabilities through APIs rather than forcing agents to "click" through human interfaces, and fundamentally reshape organizational hierarchies. The traditional corporate structure exists to handle human cognitive limitations and coordination costs, but AI agents don't have these constraints. This shift moves human roles from execution toward ownership (defining success and setting constraints) and verification (auditing outputs and handling exceptions). The article provides concrete examples from Stanford's Biomni system and MIT's Cheeseman Lab showing thousand-fold productivity improvements when systems are properly restructured for agents, far exceeding the modest 0.5% productivity gains predicted by models that assume AI simply automates existing tasks. Implementation requires four concrete changes: converting policies to machine-readable formats, creating programmatic interfaces for agent access, redefining human roles around ownership and verification, and implementing independent verification systems with rule-based alerts and approval gates.
